PARIS | FRANCE

Discover Boulangeriez

Too little has been said about the marvels of a croissant’s anatomy - feuilleté, buttery and a shower of flakes.

Xuan coined the term “discover boulangerie” as a response to my approach to food. If mine was an excel sheet first sorted by category, then by arrondisement, his was a gamble, a happy find if we’re lucky or a good laugh if not.

We spent our first few mornings in Paris boulangerie hopping, but eventually settled on our favourite one. Boulangerie Utopie occupies a tiny corner in the République neighbourhood and significant real estate in my mind still. Surely every viennoiserie, pain and pâtisserie off their shelves are baked to buttery perfection! However, if you had to choose, their OG croissant, sencha sourdough with ritz soufflé (puffed rice, I know.) and lime sesame tart floored us. They are a takeout situation, and as we sat long the kerb stuffing our faces, baker Erwan Blanche would pop out and offer us un café. Breakfast couldn't be more perfect.

Lunch is a no-frills affair at Mokonuts. Run by a Japanese-Lebanese couple Moko and Omar, dining at this tiny café is almost like visiting their private home. The space is tight - plenty of opportunity to ogle at your neighbours' tables and murmur to your companion, "Let's get that, that, and oh, that looks great too...". Jars of preserves and bottles of nattys hug the walls, leaving a nook reserved for a chalkboard with the daily specials scribbled on. Greeting us with a cheery bonjour, Moko comes along and translates the menu. It's Middle Eastern cuisine like you've never experienced before, prepared by Omar's deft hand. As we sat there deliriously full, Omar approaches and recommends every dessert on the menu, lovingly crafted by his wife. You could do worse than obliging.

Belt loops perpetually loosened from the day's feasting, we skipped a few dinners in Paris. Septime La Cave, however, was one we made an exception for. Bartender Pharaoh kept us happily drunk all night, bringing us choice reds and oranges from the rotating menu, paired with small plates that beg for seconds. Go early to grab a seat, or don't and just stand with the rest; either way your flushed faces and satisfied bellies would attest to a night well spent in the company of La Cave.
